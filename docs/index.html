Supplementary data for paper: A pilot study of linear metrics for previously-defined type-varieties of Gary dart points

Robert Z. Selden, Jr.; Christian S. Hoggard; and John E. Dockall

2020-05-09

Preface

Reproducibility—the ability to recompute results—and replicability—the chances other experimenters will achieve a consistent result—are two foundational characteristics of successful scientific research (Leek and Peng 2015).

This volume is written in Markdown, and includes all analysis code employed in the study, providing a means for others to reproduce (exactly) those results discussed and expounded upon in the following chapters. The reproducible nature of this undertaking provides a means for others to critically assess and evaluate the various analytical components of this study (Gray and Marwick 2019; Peng 2011; Gandrud 2014), which is a necessary requirement for the production of reliable knowledge. Typology is the topic of this particular undertaking, and typological assignments provide the requisite foundation for many of the assumptions, inferences, and interpretations that archaeologists regularly make. Diagnostic types—whether functional, decorative, morphological, or chronological (O’Brien and Lyman 1999)—are of considerable import in formulating archaeological interpretations. There have been no shortage of studies related to discussions of interobserver error in the assignment of archaeological types (Fish 1976, 1978; Schiffer 1976; Whittaker, Caulkins, and Kamp 1998), and while the lack of consistency in the application of types among investigators is recognized as problematic, it is expected that interobserver error and lack of consistent application is more pronounced at the level of the type-variety.

Diagnostic artifact types provide the analytical framework upon which much of our knowledge of the past is based. The focus of this particular study lies one level of abstraction beneath that of the diagnostic type, upon the many (40+) previously-defined type-varieties for Gary dart points. Given the ubiquity of Gary dart points, and the wide range of morphological variability in the type, it is not at all surprising that in at least one instance, a type-variety (var. Beaver Tail Stem) (Lewis and Kneberg 1947, 12–18) was posited seven years before the formal definition of the Gary dart point was advanced (Suhm, Krieger, and Jelks 1954).

This exploratory study of linear metrics includes those Gary dart point type-varieties advanced at the Poverty Point, Cooper, and Means sites, providing the requisite analytical foundation needed to outline a basic archaeological epistemology. Further work will be needed to expand upon this study, which provides a basic data-driven approach to assigning the previously-posited type-varities. This effort does not delve into the various sub-type-varieties (i.e., var. Camden subgroup A, var. Camden subgroup B), as it is focused only upon the type-varieties that are no more than one level of abstraction below that of the Gary dart point. A subsequent geometric morphometric study will be used to test and refine those hypotheses advanced by this exploratory study, while others will require additional data collection to incorporate the Gary dart points from the Jaketown (MS), Miller (TX), Yarbrough (TX), Lake George (MS), and Scott (OK) sites.
